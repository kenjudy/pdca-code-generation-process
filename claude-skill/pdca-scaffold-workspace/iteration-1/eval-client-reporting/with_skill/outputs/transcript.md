## Layer 1: Task Anatomy

**Questions asked:**
- What is the task broken into its smallest distinct steps?
- What inputs does each step consume and what does it produce?
- Which steps are the biggest time sinks or failure points?

**Simulated persona and reasoning:**
Maya is a mid-level engagement manager at a professional services firm managing 2-3 active
client accounts. She coordinates across internal tools and is the primary author of weekly
status reports. She is not an engineer. She is competent but time-pressured.

**Generated answers:**

Steps identified:
1. Monday: Pull TargetProcess export (velocity, open bugs, milestone status)
2. Monday: Export BillQuick billable hours by role for prior week
3. Tuesday AM: Pull Jira blocked/completed/new-high-priority tickets
4. Tuesday AM: Cross-reference all three sources, note discrepancies
5. Tuesday PM: Draft status report (exec summary, metrics, accomplishments, milestones, risks)
6. Wednesday EOD: Send draft to account lead Sarah for review
7. Thursday: Chase Sarah if needed; incorporate feedback
8. Friday by 10am: Final polish and send to client

Inputs/outputs:
- Steps 1-3 produce: raw CSV exports and Jira web data
- Step 4 produces: annotated discrepancy notes
- Step 5 produces: draft Google Doc status report
- Steps 6-7 produce: reviewed/marked-up report
- Step 8 produces: sent PDF to client

Failure points identified by reasoning through the stated problem:
- Data cross-referencing: hours and sprint velocity often misalign due to exceptions
- Risk section: consistently weak, one-liners without mitigation or owner
- Review step: Sarah receives draft Wednesday evening and returns comments Thursday afternoon,
  leaving under 24 hours for finalization

**Design decisions from Layer 1:**
- The process needs explicit structure for anomaly detection before drafting
- The risks section needs a required-field schema (not free-form prose)
- Sarah's review window needs to be protected by a gate that enforces timing

## Layer 2: Human vs. AI Responsibility

**Questions asked:**
- What decisions require human judgment and cannot be delegated?
- What can AI do well in this workflow?
- Where are the handoff points?

**Reasoning:**
The key tension is: AI can process and draft quickly, but the political/client-relationship
judgment that determines what goes in the report and how it's framed must stay human.
The task description specifically mentioned that risks get "undersold" — which is a quality
problem that AI can help with structurally (requiring all four risk fields) but not
politically (deciding what severity is appropriate given client relationship).

**Generated answers:**

Human owns:
- Risk severity confirmation for any High-severity risk
- Deciding whether to surface sensitive issues to client or handle internally
- The final send action (never AI)
- Interpreting data anomalies with contextual knowledge (e.g., hours spike due to offsite)
- Narrative tone for bad-news weeks

AI does well:
- Parsing and structuring the three CSV/Jira sources
- Generating a risks section with all four required fields from Jira blocked items + anomalies
- Drafting accomplishments from completed tickets
- Flagging discrepancies (pure computation: ratio comparison)
- Drafting review-request email to Sarah with risk summary
- Consistent formatting

Handoffs:
- Human to AI: raw exports + contextual notes
- AI to human (Gate 1): anomaly report for review
- Human to AI (cleared): authorization to draft
- AI to human (Gate 2): complete draft for approval
- Human sends: review request to Sarah
- Human to AI: Sarah's comments
- AI to human (Gate 3): revised draft for confirmation
- Human to AI (Gate 4 approval): authorization to format final
- Human sends: final report to client

**Design decisions from Layer 2:**
- Four gates map directly to the four handoff moments
- High-severity risks always require explicit human confirmation — they never auto-include
- "Human sends" is explicitly stated in phase prompts, not just implied

## Layer 3: Quality and Completeness

**Questions asked:**
- What does "good" look like for this output?
- What does "bad" look like — the failure modes to prevent?
- What is the minimum viable output if time is short?

**Reasoning:**
The two named failure modes from the task description were: (1) risks get undersold, and
(2) review gets rushed. "Good" is defined in opposition to these two failure modes.
I also reasoned that a consulting client report has a specific client-facing persona requirement:
the reader is a VP-level executive who should not need to parse technical terms.

**Generated answers:**

Good looks like:
- Every risk has all four fields: description, severity, mitigation, owner
- Metrics table matches all three source systems (client can verify billing)
- Exec summary is 3-4 sentences, no jargon
- Sarah had 24+ hours with the draft
- No surprises in the report (Sarah knew all risks before client saw them)
- Delivered before Friday noon

Bad looks like:
- "Monitoring situation" with no mitigation or owner
- Hours in report don't match billing system (reconciliation risk)
- Sarah reviewed at 11:45am for an 11:59am send
- Client saw the report before Sarah
- Exec summary contains acronyms ("we completed the CI/CD pipeline migration")

Minimum viable:
- Metrics table (accurate, sourced)
- Risks section (at least 3 entries, all four fields)
- Executive summary (plain English)

**Design decisions from Layer 3:**
- "Zero risks" is a hard fail condition at Gate 2 — it is never acceptable
- "Hours must trace to source" is a specific measurable criterion, not just "accurate"
- The minimum viable definition goes into quality-gates.md so it's available under pressure
- A plain-English check is built into Gate 2 and Gate 3 criteria

## Layer 4: Intervention Triggers

**Questions asked:**
- When should AI stop and wait for human input before proceeding?
- What conditions mean "do not proceed, escalate"?
- What is the cadence — when in the week does each gate occur?

**Reasoning:**
Intervention triggers need to be specific enough to be unambiguous. "When something looks wrong"
is not a trigger. I derived triggers from the failure modes and the data discrepancy patterns
described implicitly in the task. The 10% threshold is a reasonable operational heuristic for
consulting contexts — small variances are noise, large variances need explanation.

The Thursday 3pm escalation rule is specific and important: sending a second email to Sarah
when she is already late responding only creates more email noise. Calling is the right action.
This is a working agreement, not a phase prompt — it belongs in working-agreements.md.

**Generated answers:**

Intervention triggers:
1. Data discrepancy >10% between any two source systems
2. A risk is classified High severity (human confirms before inclusion)
3. A new P0/P1 Jira bug appears that was not in any prior report
4. Any data source export is empty
5. Risks section produces zero entries (always an error condition)

Do not proceed / escalate:
- Two or more data sources unavailable simultaneously
- Thursday 3pm and Sarah has not responded (call, don't email)

Gate cadence:
- Gate 1: Tuesday PM (after data pull and anomaly review)
- Gate 2: Wednesday AM (before review request sent to Sarah)
- Gate 3: Thursday PM (after Sarah's feedback incorporated)
- Gate 4: Friday 10am (before client send)

**Design decisions from Layer 4:**
- Gates are time-anchored, not just sequential — this creates accountability
- The Thursday escalation rule is elevated to a non-negotiable in working-agreements.md
- "Zero risks blocks Gate 2" is stated explicitly in quality-gates.md as a hard fail

## Layer 5: Learning Loop

**Questions asked:**
- After each cycle, what do you want to learn?
- How will you capture that learning?
- What would cause you to change the process itself?

**Reasoning:**
The learning loop must be lightweight enough that it actually gets done each week. A 10-minute
retro after sending the report is realistic; a 45-minute retrospective is not. The four questions
map directly to the named failure modes (risk quality, data accuracy, deadline adherence) plus
one client-feedback signal.

The refinement trigger logic (two consecutive cycles with the same failure) is borrowed from
standard SPC reasoning — one instance is noise, two is a pattern worth acting on.

**Generated answers:**

Learn each cycle:
1. How many risks needed substantive rewriting after Sarah's review?
   (Indicates whether AI risk calibration is matching Maya's and Sarah's judgment)
2. Did AI catch all data discrepancies, or did human find ones AI missed?
3. Did we hit Friday noon? Which gate was the bottleneck?
4. One-word client reaction if known

Capture method:
- cycle-log.md appended after each cycle, with the retro format defined in quality-gates.md
- Log lives alongside the skill in the project directory

Change triggers:
- Risk rewrites >= 2 for two consecutive cycles: adjust severity guidance in phase-prompts.md
- Same gate caused late delivery two consecutive cycles: adjust timing or criteria for that gate
- Client reaction is Negative two consecutive cycles: escalate to account lead for process review

**Design decisions from Layer 5:**
- Retro is built into the ACT phase prompt — it is not optional
- The format is in quality-gates.md so it is findable at the right moment (after Gate 4)
- Refinement is a diff, not a rewrite — the anti-drift rule from pdca-scaffold applies
